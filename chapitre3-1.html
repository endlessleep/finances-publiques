3.1 — Fiscal Dominance

Chapter 3: Fiscal-Monetary Policy Nexus

What is Fiscal Dominance?

Definition

Fiscal dominance happens when a country's fiscal policy (government spending and borrowing) controls or limits its monetary policy (central bank actions), often making it hard for the central bank to manage inflation and keep prices stable.

In short, fiscal dominance is a situation where government debt pressures force central banks to put fiscal needs before their own goals, which can lead to inflation and hurt economic stability.

In a well-working economy, the central bank sets interest rates on its own to control inflation, while the government manages its spending and revenue responsibly. Fiscal dominance breaks this setup: when the government spends or borrows too much, the central bank has no choice but to go along — usually by printing money (monetization) or keeping interest rates artificially low — to avoid a debt crisis.

This is one of the most important ideas where monetary and fiscal policy meet, and it has real effects in the real world: hyperinflation in Zimbabwe, the European debt crisis in 2010-2012, and even the recent post-Covid inflation spike can all be explained using the idea of fiscal dominance.

The Dynamic Budget Constraint

To understand fiscal dominance properly, we need the government's dynamic budget constraint — the equation that shows how public debt changes over time.

Key Equation — Discrete Time

$b_{t+1} = \frac{d_t - \mu_t h_t}{(1+\pi_t)(1+g_t)} + \frac{1+r_t}{1+g_t} b_t$

Key Equation — Continuous Time (Simplified)

$\dot{b}_t \equiv d_t - \mu_t h_t + (r_t - g_t)b_t$

Variable Meaning
$b_t$ Debt-to-GDP ratio at time $t$
$d_t$ Primary deficit (government spending minus revenue, excluding interest payments)
$\mu_t h_t$ Seigniorage — revenue the central bank generates by printing money ($\mu_t$ = money growth rate, $h_t$ = monetary base / GDP)
$\pi_t$ Inflation rate
$g_t$ Real GDP growth rate
$r_t$ Real interest rate on government debt

The key insight is in the term $(r_t - g_t)$ — the difference between the real interest rate and the growth rate. This is the snowball effect:

Case 1

📈 When $r > g$

The interest rate is higher than growth → the debt grows faster than the economy. This is the explosive path: even if the government balances its main budget, the debt ratio keeps rising because interest payments grow faster than GDP.

Primary deficits must be covered by seigniorage to make the budget work. Otherwise, the debt ratio goes on an explosive path.

Case 2

📉 When $r < g$

Growth is higher than the interest rate → the government faces a soft budget constraint. The debt ratio can stabilize even with primary deficits not covered by seigniorage, because GDP growth "waters down" the existing debt.

This is the good scenario. Darby (1983) called it "Some Pleasant Monetarist Arithmetic" — in reality, $r < g$ often lasts for long periods.

💡 What can be adjusted? When $r > g$, the government has only two options: increase seigniorage (forcing the central bank to print money → inflation) or reduce the primary deficit (through higher taxes or less spending). Fiscal dominance happens when the government refuses to fix the deficit and forces the central bank to create seigniorage instead.

The Four Policy Regimes

How do monetary policy and fiscal policy work together? Sargent & Wallace (1981), Leeper (1991), and Sims (1994) showed that the way these two policies interact can be sorted into four different regimes, depending on whether each policy is "active" (strongly pursuing its own goals) or "passive" (adjusting to help the other).

✅ Textbook Case

MP Active + FP Passive

The ideal regime in modern macroeconomics. The central bank actively fights for price stability by reacting strongly enough to inflation (Taylor rule). Fiscal policy passively adjusts taxes and spending to keep debt under control.

Result: stable inflation and manageable debt. This is how most OECD countries try to operate.

⚠️ Fiscal Dominance

MP Passive + FP Active

Fiscal policy refuses to change taxes or spending. The government runs ongoing deficits that are not fully paid for by future taxes. The central bank is forced to let the money supply grow (monetization) to keep the budget afloat.

Result: inflation depends on the fiscal situation, not on monetary policy. The central bank loses control over prices.

❓ Indeterminacy

MP Passive + FP Passive

Neither policy takes the lead. Nobody anchors the price level. Many outcomes are possible, and inflation is indeterminate — the economy can drift to any price level without anything to stabilize it.

💣 Explosive

MP Active + FP Active

Both policies try to control the economy at the same time. The central bank raises rates to fight inflation while the government keeps spending. These conflicting signals create instability and can lead to explosive outcomes.

For exams: learn the 2×2 matrix by heart. The key question is who adjusts: in the textbook case, fiscal policy adjusts to monetary policy; in fiscal dominance, monetary policy adjusts to fiscal policy. The other two corners (both passive, both active) lead to bad outcomes.

Why is Fiscal Dominance Bad?

Fiscal dominance is not just a theoretical idea — it has caused some of the worst economic disasters in history. There are four main ways fiscal dominance hurts the economy:

Consequence 1

Hyperinflation — The Most Extreme Outcome

When the central bank must create seigniorage to cover government costs, this can — in extreme but historically quite common cases — lead to hyperinflation (Cagan, 1956). The monetary system breaks down completely: prices double every few days or weeks, the currency becomes worthless, and the economy goes back to barter.

Historical examples: Germany (1923), Zimbabwe (2008), Venezuela (2017-present), Hungary (1946 — the worst hyperinflation ever recorded).

Consequence 2

Currency Crises

Fiscal dominance can break fixed exchange rate systems and cause higher inflation and ongoing drops in output. Krugman (1979) created a model explaining the collapse of the Bretton-Woods system through fiscal dominance: the central bank wants to fix the exchange rate, but must also print money for government deficits. This leads to a slow loss of foreign exchange reserves and an unavoidable currency crisis.

Central Bank Balance Sheet (Krugman Model)
Assets Liabilities
FX reserves ($e \cdot B_{F,t}$) Monetary Base ($M_{0,t}$)
Government bonds ($B_{H,t}$)

As the central bank buys more government bonds (monetization), it must sell foreign reserves to keep the exchange rate fixed. When reserves run out → the peg collapses → currency crash.

Consequence 3

Inability to Control Inflation — "Some Unpleasant Monetarist Arithmetic"

Sargent & Wallace (1981) showed a paradoxical result: in an economy where the quantity theory of money holds but monetary policy is dominated by fiscal needs (with $r > g$), lower money supply growth and inflation at present means higher inflation in the future.

Why? Because tighter money today means more government bonds are issued instead of being printed as money → higher future debt → even more money printing needed later. And if money demand depends on expected inflation, less money supply growth may even lead to higher inflation now, because people expect future monetization.

Consequence 4

Self-Fulfilling Sovereign Debt Crises

Darby (1983) noted that $r < g$ often lasts for long periods — "Some Pleasant Monetarist Arithmetic". But governments tend to rely too much on such good conditions. Neither $g$ nor $r$ are fully in the government's control:

  • Growth could become negative during crisis times.
  • If government debt exceeds some threshold, risk premiums increase, causing $r > g$.
  • This starts a self-fulfilling cycle: investors fear default → they demand higher yields → higher yields make the debt worse → which confirms the initial fear.
🚨 Real-world example: During the 2010-2012 European sovereign debt crisis, 10-year government bond yields jumped sharply for weaker Eurozone countries. Greece reached ~30%, Portugal ~15%, Ireland ~14%, Italy and Spain ~7%. These were typical self-fulfilling patterns: rising yields → worse fiscal position → even higher yields. Only the ECB stepping in ("whatever it takes" — Draghi, 2012) stopped the spiral.

The Solution: Prohibition of Monetary Financing

How do modern economies prevent fiscal dominance? Through institutional design:

  1. Central bank independence

    Central banks must be independent from the government — both politically (no instructions from politicians) and financially (no dependence on government funding). They must have a clear mandate focused on price stability as their main goal.

  2. Legal prohibition of monetary financing

    The EU enshrines this in Article 123 TFEU (ex Art. 101 TEC): "Overdraft facilities or any other type of credit facility with the ECB or national central banks in favour of Union institutions, central governments, regional, local or other public authorities shall be prohibited, as shall the purchase directly from them of debt instruments."

  3. Seigniorage as by-product, not policy tool

    Seigniorage is treated as a welcome by-product helping to keep the central bank financially independent, but it is generally low (less than 0.3% of GDP, typically even lower) in a low-inflation environment.

⚠️ First-best vs. second-best: In theory, the best solution would be coordination of monetary and fiscal policy, but in practice the second-best solution of fully independent monetary policy is used instead. Why? Because coordination needs trust, and political pressures make it too tempting for governments to pressure central banks.

Fiscal dominance = fiscal policy controls monetary policy. It arises when the government refuses to adjust its deficits and forces the central bank to monetize debt. The dynamic budget constraint shows that when $r > g$, this leads to explosive debt or inflation. The solution is institutional: independent central banks with a price stability mandate and a legal prohibition of monetary financing.
