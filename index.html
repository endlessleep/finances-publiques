1.1 — What Is Public Finance?

Chapter 1: Introduction — Economic Rationale for the Government

What is Public Finance?

Definition

Public finance (also called public economics) is the branch of economics that studies the role of government in the economy. It examines how governments raise revenue (mainly through taxation), how they spend that revenue (public goods, social transfers, infrastructure), and how these decisions affect the well-being of individuals and the efficiency of markets.

Why is this field so important? Because government is not some abstract, distant entity — it is instrumental in virtually every aspect of economic life. Whether you pay taxes on your salary, receive healthcare from a public hospital, drive on a publicly-funded road, or benefit from a pension when you retire, the government is directly involved. Understanding public finance means understanding the economic logic — and the trade-offs — behind all of these interventions.

Public finance, in practice, revolves around three fundamental types of questions. These three "lenses" are the intellectual pillars of the entire course, and every topic we study can be viewed through one or more of them.

The Three Fundamental Questions

Question 1

🔍 Positive

How do government policies actually affect the economy?

This is the descriptive approach: we look at the real world and measure what happens when a policy is implemented. We don't judge whether the outcome is "good" or "bad" — we simply observe and quantify.

Example: "Do higher income taxes reduce the number of hours people work?" — This is a factual, measurable question.

Question 2

⚖️ Normative

How should government policies be designed to maximize social welfare?

This is the prescriptive approach: given what we know from positive analysis, what is the optimal policy? This requires making value judgments about what "welfare" means and whose welfare matters.

Example: "Should the top marginal tax rate be 50% or 70%?" — This depends on your view of fairness, efficiency, and individual freedom.

Question 3

🏛️ Political Economy

Why do governments choose to intervene the way that they do?

This is the explanatory approach: even if we know the "optimal" policy, governments often choose something different. Why? Because of political incentives, lobbying, voter preferences, institutional constraints, and the self-interest of politicians.

Example: "Why do governments subsidize agriculture even when economists say it's inefficient?" — Because farmers are a concentrated, politically active group.

🎯 Key insight: Every policy debate in public finance can be decomposed into these three questions. First, we analyze what is (positive). Then, we discuss what should be (normative). Finally, we explain why reality diverges from the ideal (political economy). Mastering this distinction is essential for the exam.

Positive Public Economics

Positive Analysis

Positive public economics is the analysis of how things really are. It deals with facts, data, and empirical evidence — not with opinions or value judgments. The goal is to understand the actual effects of government policies on the economy and on individuals' behaviour.

This branch of public economics is primarily empirical, meaning it relies heavily on data, statistical methods, and econometric techniques to establish causal relationships. For instance, a positive economist might use a natural experiment (like a tax reform that affects only certain regions) to measure how tax changes influence labor supply.

Positive public economics is also deeply connected to other fields of economics:

  • Behavioral economics — because people don't always act rationally; they respond to framing, defaults, and psychological biases.
  • Labor economics — because taxes on labor directly affect wages, hours worked, and job-seeking behavior.
  • Political economy — because understanding why policies exist helps us understand the consequences of those policies.
  • International tax — because in a globalized world, capital and sometimes labor can move across borders to avoid domestic taxes.

One crucial distinction in positive analysis is between direct effects and indirect (unintended) effects. When a government introduces a new tax on sugary drinks, the direct effect is that the price of those drinks goes up and consumption falls. But there may also be indirect effects: consumers may switch to artificial sweeteners (which have their own health risks), small businesses may lose revenue, or people may cross the border to buy cheaper drinks elsewhere. A good positive analysis accounts for both.

⚠️ Important: Positive analysis is a necessary first step before normative analysis. You cannot decide what should be done without first understanding what is happening. This is why empirical research is the foundation of evidence-based policymaking.

Normative Public Economics

Normative Analysis

Normative public economics is the analysis of how things should be. It asks: given what we know about the economy, what is the best or most desirable policy? Unlike positive economics, normative economics necessarily involves value judgments — about fairness, justice, freedom, and social welfare.

This branch of public economics is primarily theoretical. It uses mathematical models of utility, welfare, and social choice to derive optimal policies. For example, a normative economist might derive the "optimal income tax schedule" — the tax rates that maximize a social welfare function — given assumptions about how people respond to taxes (which we learn from positive analysis).

At the heart of normative public economics lies a fundamental tension:

Approach A

🗽 Laissez-Faire

Individuals know what's best for themselves. The government should intervene as little as possible and let markets allocate resources freely. People are rational, and their choices reveal their true preferences.

Implication: Low taxes, minimal regulation, small government.

Approach B

🏛️ Paternalism

People sometimes make choices that are bad for themselves (smoking, under-saving for retirement, ignoring health risks). The government can and should "nudge" or even mandate certain behaviors to improve people's own welfare.

Implication: Higher taxes on "sin goods", mandatory pensions, public healthcare.

Which approach is "right"? That depends on your fundamental assumptions: your view of human rationality, your definition of welfare (is it what people want, or what's good for them?), your concept of justice (equal outcomes? equal opportunities?), and the shape of social welfare functions you believe in.

Normative economics also deals with two types of failures that justify government intervention:

  • Market failures — situations where competitive markets fail to produce efficient outcomes (externalities, public goods, imperfect information, etc.). These are the classical justifications for intervention.
  • Individual failures — situations where people fail to act in their own best interest, even when markets work perfectly (self-control problems, cognitive biases, procrastination). These are the newer, behavioral justifications for paternalistic policies.

The line between positive and normative economics is not always sharp in practice. Many policy debates involve a mix of factual disagreements ("Do minimum wages cause unemployment?") and value disagreements ("Is some unemployment acceptable if it means higher wages for the poorest?"). Learning to distinguish these two types of disagreement is a core skill in public economics.

Should the Government Intervene?

Not everything should be done by the government, and not everything should be left to the market. In practice, most people — regardless of political ideology — agree that government intervention falls on a spectrum. We can organize this spectrum into three broad categories:

Category 1

✅ Nearly Universal Agreement: YES

These are things that virtually everyone agrees must be done by the government. They typically involve activities that require coercive authority, coordination at scale, or natural monopoly characteristics.

Examples: National defense, police, justice system, legislation, diplomacy, visa policy, census/data collection, birth certificates, land registry, company registry, driver's licenses.

Category 2

🔄 Debatable

These are the areas that are actively debated in public finance. Reasonable people disagree about whether the government should be involved, and if so, how much. This is where most of the course content lives.

Examples: Healthcare, pensions, unemployment insurance, heavy redistribution, subsidies for trains/transport, education, housing policy, beer brewing (historically state-run in some countries).

Category 3

❌ Nearly Universal Agreement: NO

These are things that virtually everyone agrees the government should not regulate. They involve personal choices that do not create significant externalities or market failures.

Examples: What shirts to wear, whether to eat meat, how long to sleep, whether to exercise.

On an exam, if you're asked "should the government intervene in X?", never answer with a simple yes or no. Instead, discuss which category X falls into, identify the market failure (if any), assess the costs and benefits of intervention, and consider whether government failure might make things worse. This systematic approach demonstrates analytical thinking.

Why Study Public Finance?

There are three compelling reasons to study public finance, each appealing to a different motivation:

  1. Practical Relevance

    Government is one of the largest players in any modern economy. Consider just one example: the Czech Republic (Czechia) has around 18,000 public entities — schools, hospitals, municipalities, infrastructure agencies — and employs roughly 450,000 people directly (and about 1 million people when you include state-owned enterprises and indirect employment). This is not a minor sector of the economy — it's a massive organizational structure that touches the lives of every citizen.

    Across the OECD (the club of wealthy democracies), tax revenue averages around 34% of GDP. This means that roughly one-third of all economic activity is channelled through the government. And this proportion has increased dramatically over the past 100 years — at the start of the 20th century, government spending in most countries was below 10% of GDP. Understanding this enormous growth in government is one of the key puzzles of public finance.

    Government spending is typically on par with or even higher than tax revenue, which means many governments run budget deficits. This raises further questions about public debt, fiscal sustainability, and intergenerational fairness.

  2. Policy Impact

    Public finance is not just an academic exercise — it has direct, immediate consequences for millions of people. When a policymaker decides how to design social transfers, unemployment benefits, or the tax system, those decisions shape people's income, health, opportunities, and quality of life.

    The fundamental question is: What can policymakers do to increase the welfare of society? But answering this requires first answering a deeper question: How do we measure social welfare? Is it GDP per capita? Is it the welfare of the poorest person? Is it average life satisfaction? Different measurement choices lead to different policy recommendations.

  3. Philosophy & Politics

    At its deepest level, public finance is a branch of political philosophy. It asks: When is the government the best way to organize ourselves? In which areas? Can people take good care of themselves (laissez-faire), or do they need guidance (paternalism)?

    It also engages with questions of redistribution and inequality: Should we care about inequality of outcomes (how much money people end up with) or inequality of opportunity (whether everyone starts with a fair chance)? And how should we collectively decide what the public sector does — through voting, delegation, direct democracy, or technocratic expertise?

As a student in economics, you will encounter public finance in your career whether you work in government, consulting, NGOs, or the private sector. Tax policy affects corporate decision-making. Public spending affects demand and employment. Social transfers affect labor supply. Understanding these mechanisms gives you a competitive edge in any economic role.
