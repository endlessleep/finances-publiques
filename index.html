1.1 — What Is Public Finance?

Chapter 1: Introduction — Economic Rationale for the Government

What is Public Finance?

Definition

Public finance (also called public economics) is the part of economics that studies the role of government in the economy. It looks at how governments collect money (mostly through taxes), how they spend that money (public goods, social transfers, infrastructure), and how these choices affect people's lives and the way markets work.

Why is this field so important? Because the government is not something distant — it is involved in almost every part of economic life. When you pay taxes on your salary, get healthcare from a public hospital, drive on a public road, or receive a pension when you retire — the government is directly involved. Understanding public finance means understanding the reasons — and the trade-offs — behind all of these decisions.

In practice, public finance is built around three key types of questions. These three approaches are the main ideas of the course, and every topic can be looked at through one or more of them.

The Three Fundamental Questions

Question 1

🔍 Positive

How do government policies actually affect the economy?

This is the descriptive approach: we look at the real world and measure what happens when a policy is introduced. We don't judge if the result is "good" or "bad" — we just observe and measure.

Example: "Do higher income taxes make people work fewer hours?" — This is a factual question that can be measured.

Question 2

⚖️ Normative

How should government policies be designed to maximize social welfare?

This is about recommendations: based on what we know from positive analysis, what is the best policy? This requires value judgments — deciding what "welfare" means and whose welfare matters most.

Example: "Should the top tax rate be 50% or 70%?" — The answer depends on your view of fairness, efficiency, and personal freedom.

Question 3

🏛️ Political Economy

Why do governments choose to intervene the way that they do?

Even if we know the "best" policy, governments often choose something different. Why? Because of political pressure, lobbying, what voters want, rules of the system, and the personal interests of politicians.

Example: "Why do governments subsidize agriculture even when economists say it's inefficient?" — Because farmers are a concentrated, politically active group.

🎯 Key insight: Every policy debate in public finance can be broken down into these three questions. First, we look at what is (positive). Then, we discuss what should be (normative). Finally, we explain why reality is different from the ideal (political economy). Understanding this difference is essential for the exam.

Positive Public Economics

Positive Analysis

Positive public economics is the analysis of how things really are. It deals with facts, data, and empirical evidence — not with opinions or value judgments. The goal is to understand the actual effects of government policies on the economy and on individuals' behaviour.

This part of public economics is mainly based on data. It uses statistics and econometric methods to find cause-and-effect relationships. For example, an economist might study a tax reform that only affects certain regions to measure how tax changes affect how much people work.

Positive public economics is closely linked to other areas of economics:

  • Behavioral economics — because people don't always act rationally; they react to how choices are presented and to psychological biases.
  • Labor economics — because taxes on work directly affect wages, hours worked, and job search.
  • Political economy — because understanding why policies exist helps us understand their effects.
  • International tax — because in today's world, money and workers can move across borders to avoid taxes.

One crucial distinction in positive analysis is between direct effects and indirect (unintended) effects. When a government introduces a new tax on sugary drinks, the direct effect is that the price of those drinks goes up and consumption falls. But there may also be indirect effects: consumers may switch to artificial sweeteners (which have their own health risks), small businesses may lose revenue, or people may cross the border to buy cheaper drinks elsewhere. A good positive analysis accounts for both.

⚠️ Important: Positive analysis is a necessary first step before normative analysis. You cannot decide what should be done without first understanding what is happening. This is why empirical research is the foundation of evidence-based policymaking.

Normative Public Economics

Normative Analysis

Normative public economics is the analysis of how things should be. It asks: given what we know about the economy, what is the best or most desirable policy? Unlike positive economics, normative economics necessarily involves value judgments — about fairness, justice, freedom, and social welfare.

This part of public economics is mainly theoretical. It uses mathematical models to find the best possible policies. For example, a normative economist might try to find the "best income tax rates" — the rates that maximize social welfare — based on what we know about how people react to taxes (from positive analysis).

At the center of normative economics, there is a key tension:

Approach A

🗽 Laissez-Faire

Individuals know what's best for themselves. The government should intervene as little as possible and let markets allocate resources freely. People are rational, and their choices reveal their true preferences.

Implication: Low taxes, minimal regulation, small government.

Approach B

🏛️ Paternalism

People sometimes make choices that are bad for themselves (smoking, under-saving for retirement, ignoring health risks). The government can and should "nudge" or even mandate certain behaviors to improve people's own welfare.

Implication: Higher taxes on "sin goods", mandatory pensions, public healthcare.

Which approach is "right"? It depends on your basic assumptions: do you think people are rational? Is welfare what people want, or what is good for them? Should we aim for equal outcomes or equal opportunities? The answer shapes everything.

Normative economics also looks at two types of failures that justify government intervention:

  • Market failures — when markets don't produce efficient results (externalities, public goods, lack of information, etc.). These are the classic reasons for government intervention.
  • Individual failures — when people don't act in their own best interest, even when markets work well (lack of self-control, biases, procrastination). These are the newer, behavioral reasons for paternalistic policies.

In practice, the line between positive and normative economics is not always clear. Many policy debates mix factual questions ("Do minimum wages cause unemployment?") with value questions ("Is some unemployment OK if it means higher wages for the poorest?"). Learning to tell these two types of disagreement apart is a key skill in public economics.

Should the Government Intervene?

Not everything should be done by the government, and not everything should be left to the market. In practice, most people — no matter their political views — agree that government intervention is a matter of degree. We can organize it into three categories:

Category 1

✅ Nearly Universal Agreement: YES

These are things that almost everyone agrees must be done by the government. They usually need the power of the state, large-scale organization, or are natural monopolies.

Examples: National defense, police, justice system, laws, diplomacy, visa policy, census, birth certificates, land registry, company registry, driver's licenses.

Category 2

🔄 Debatable

These are the areas that are actively debated in public finance. Reasonable people disagree about whether the government should be involved, and if so, how much. This is where most of the course content lives.

Examples: Healthcare, pensions, unemployment insurance, heavy redistribution, subsidies for trains/transport, education, housing policy, beer brewing (historically state-run in some countries).

Category 3

❌ Nearly Universal Agreement: NO

These are things that virtually everyone agrees the government should not regulate. They involve personal choices that do not create significant externalities or market failures.

Examples: What shirts to wear, whether to eat meat, how long to sleep, whether to exercise.

On an exam, if you're asked "should the government intervene in X?", never answer with a simple yes or no. Instead, discuss which category X falls into, identify the market failure (if any), weigh the costs and benefits, and think about whether government action might actually make things worse. This structured approach shows good analysis.

Why Study Public Finance?

There are three good reasons to study public finance:

  1. Practical Relevance

    Government is one of the largest players in any modern economy. Consider just one example: the Czech Republic (Czechia) has around 18,000 public entities — schools, hospitals, municipalities, infrastructure agencies — and employs roughly 450,000 people directly (and about 1 million people when you include state-owned companies). This is not a small part of the economy — it's huge and it affects every citizen.

    Across the OECD (the group of rich democracies), tax revenue averages around 34% of GDP. This means about one-third of all economic activity goes through the government. And this share has grown a lot over the past 100 years — at the start of the 20th century, government spending was below 10% of GDP in most countries. Understanding this big growth in government is one of the key questions of public finance.

    Government spending is typically on par with or even higher than tax revenue, which means many governments run budget deficits. This raises further questions about public debt, fiscal sustainability, and intergenerational fairness.

  2. Policy Impact

    Public finance is not just theory — it has real, direct effects on millions of people. When a policymaker designs social transfers, unemployment benefits, or the tax system, those choices shape people's income, health, opportunities, and quality of life.

    The key question is: What can policymakers do to improve the well-being of society? But first we need to ask: How do we measure well-being? Is it GDP per person? The situation of the poorest people? Average life satisfaction? Different ways of measuring lead to different policy recommendations.

  3. Philosophy & Politics

    At a deeper level, public finance is about political philosophy. It asks: When is the government the best solution? In which areas? Can people take care of themselves (laissez-faire), or do they need guidance (paternalism)?

    It also deals with redistribution and inequality: Should we care about inequality of outcomes (how much money people end up with) or inequality of opportunity (whether everyone starts with a fair chance)? And how should we decide together what the government does — through voting, delegation, direct democracy, or experts?

As an economics student, you will meet public finance in your career whether you work in government, consulting, NGOs, or the private sector. Tax policy affects business decisions. Public spending affects demand and jobs. Social transfers affect how much people work. Understanding these links gives you an advantage in any economic job.
